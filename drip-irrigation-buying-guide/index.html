How to choose drip irrigation

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

To choose a drip irrigation kit, first match the kit type to your garden layout, emitter tubing for rows of plants or soaker hose for dense beds, then confirm the kit covers your square footage and connects to your outdoor faucet with minimal adapters. A timer and pressure regulator are worth the small added cost for consistent watering and fewer headaches.

What type of drip kit should I buy?

Most kits fall into two categories: emitter-based systems and soaker hoses. Emitter kits use flexible tubing with built-in or punch-in drippers that deliver water right at the root zone. They are precise and work well for vegetable gardens, raised beds, and spaced-out plants. Soaker hose kits are a simpler option, a porous hose that weeps water along its whole length. They are better for closely planted flower beds, hedges, or narrow strips of lawn, but they can water unevenly on slopes and are not easy to extend. If you plan to expand your system later, an emitter kit offers more flexibility.

How do I know what size drip kit I need?

Measure the total length of your planting area in feet and check the kit’s coverage rating. A small kit (often covering around 50 to 100 square feet) works for a couple of raised beds or a tight patio garden. Larger kits (covering several hundred square feet) are meant for full garden rows or long borders. Be practical: it is easier to buy a slightly bigger kit than you think you need because you can cap off extra tubing or leave unused emitters plugged. If your garden has multiple zones, like a sunny vegetable bed and a shady flower border, you may want two small kits or a single kit with a valve to switch between sections.

What components should a good drip kit include?

Every solid kit comes with a mainline tubing (usually half-inch or quarter-inch diameter), enough emitters or drippers for the included tubing, connectors (elbows, T-fittings, end caps), and a backflow preventer to keep garden water from flowing backward into your hose. Many also include a filter to catch debris that can clog the tiny emitters. A pressure regulator is the component most often missing from budget kits, and it makes a real difference: household water pressure can blow out the tubing or cause uneven flow. Look for a kit that includes a regulator rated for around 20 to 30 psi. If the kit lacks a timer, consider adding a simple battery-powered one for a very reasonable price, it saves water and automates your schedule.

How do I install a drip irrigation system without a professional?

Installation is straightforward and requires only a few common tools, scissors or a tubing cutter, a hole punch (if your kit uses punch-in emitters), and maybe a stake or two. Start at the faucet: attach the backflow preventer, then the filter, then the pressure regulator (if included), then the timer (optional). Connect the mainline tubing to the regulator and lay it along your planting row. Cut the tubing at the end and seal it with an end cap or a fold-over clamp. For emitter kits, punch holes at each plant and insert drippers. For soaker kits, just lay the hose near the plants and turn on the water slowly. Let the system run for 15 minutes and check for leaks or dry spots, adjust emitter placement or add extra sections if needed.

Can I use a drip kit for a vegetable garden?

Yes, even better: vegetables love consistent, deep watering, and drip irrigation avoids wet foliage that invites disease. Emitter tubing is ideal because you can space drippers exactly next to each plant or run a drip tape along rows for crops like tomatoes, peppers, and lettuce. One common mistake is using a soaker hose on a vegetable bed, it tends to water the path between rows as much as the plants. For a standard 4x8 foot raised bed, a small emitter kit with 1/4-inch micro-tubing and adjustable drippers gives you control over each plant’s water. Just make sure the kit covers the full square footage of your beds.

Should I get a timer for my drip irrigation kit?

A timer is not strictly necessary, but anyone who has forgotten to turn off the hose or gone on vacation will tell you it is worth it. A timer lets you set a schedule, say, 20 minutes every morning, and stops water waste even if you are not home. Simple battery-powered timers are widely available and very affordable. If your kit did not come with a timer, buy one that fits standard garden hoses (most do) and attach it before the backflow preventer. Look for a model with multiple zone options if you plan to expand later. Timers with rain-delay features are especially handy for climate that gets sudden downpours.

Frequently asked questions

How long should I run my drip irrigation system each time?

It depends on your soil type and plant needs. Sandy soil absorbs quickly and may need 15–20 minute runs twice a day; clay soil holds water longer so a single 30–40 minute run every couple of days is enough. A good rule is to run it for 15 minutes, check soil moisture at root depth, and adjust until the soil is moist down to 6 inches.

Can I connect multiple drip kits to one faucet?

Yes, you can use a “Y” splitter at the faucet with separate timers or valves for each kit. Just keep total flow below what your faucet supply can deliver, most outdoor spigots provide about 8 to 10 gallons per minute, and a typical drip kit uses less than 2 gpm, so you can usually run two or three kits at once.

Do drip irrigation kits work with a rain barrel?

They can, but you need enough water pressure. Rain barrels rely on gravity, so the pressure is low, often below 10 psi. Most drip kits require at least 15–20 psi for even coverage. A small pump or an elevated barrel (a few feet above the garden) can help, but for best results use a kit that specifically mentions low-pressure compatibility.

How do I winterize my drip irrigation system?

Disconnect all components from the faucet and drain any water by lifting the tubing ends. Store the timer indoors to protect the batteries and electronics from freezing. Keep the tubing protected from sunlight and rodents if possible, many people coil it up in a garage or shed for the winter.

What is the difference between 1/4-inch and 1/2-inch tubing?

1/2-inch tubing is mainline material used to carry water across the garden; it can handle higher flow and longer runs without pressure loss. 1/4-inch tubing is branch or delivery tubing used to reach individual plants from the mainline. Most kits include 1/2-inch for the main runs and 1/4-inch for the emitters or drip lines.

Can I bury drip irrigation tubing under mulch or soil?

You can bury it under a few inches of mulch to hide it and reduce evaporation, but avoid burying it deep in soil. The emitters can clog with dirt and roots, and inspection becomes difficult. A better practice is to lay tubing on the soil surface and cover lightly with mulch, which also reduces wear from sunlight.

In shortThe best drip irrigation kit is the one that matches your garden’s layout and your willingness to tweak it over a few seasons. Start small, include a timer and pressure regulator from the beginning, and give yourself room to add more tubing or emitters later. With the right kit and a bit of planning, you will water less often and see healthier plants, while saving money on your water bill every summer.
